Q1  4 marks

Peter wants to create a home network. He is a gamer who needs the fastest connection possible. His sister and mum use a laptop and tablet around the house. They live in a large house and the wireless signal must reach across the whole building.

Describe the network hardware Peter will need and explain why each item is suitable for their needs.

  • Think about what connects the network to the internet.
  • How does Peter connect his gaming PC at the fastest speed? (Wired or wireless?)
  • What device allows wireless devices to connect?
  • If the house is large, how could you ensure wireless signal reaches everywhere?
  • What does every device need installed to join a network?
Router – connects the home LAN to the internet (WAN) and routes data between devices and the internet.
Modem – converts the internet signal from analogue (telephone line) to digital so computers can use it.
Switch – Peter should connect his gaming PC via a switch (or directly) using an Ethernet cable for the fastest, most reliable connection, as wired connections have lower latency than wireless.
Wireless Access Point (WAP) – allows the laptop and tablet to connect wirelessly. As the house is large, multiple WAPs (or a mesh system) may be needed to ensure full coverage.
Network Interface Cards (NICs) – every device needs a NIC (wired or wireless) to physically connect to the network.

Q2  3 marks

Explain the difference between a hub and a switch. Why are switches preferred on modern networks?

  • What does a hub do with data it receives?
  • What does a switch do differently?
  • Which creates more unnecessary traffic on the network and why?
A hub sends any data it receives to all devices connected to it, regardless of which device the data is intended for. This creates unnecessary traffic and can slow the network down.

A switch stores the MAC address of each connected device and only sends data to the correct device. This is more efficient and reduces unnecessary data on the network.

Switches are preferred because they reduce network congestion, improve speed, and are more secure since other devices cannot see data intended for someone else.

Q3  2 marks

A student says: "A modem and a router do the same job — they both connect you to the internet."

Is this statement correct? Explain the difference between the two devices.

  • What does a modem convert? What type of signals are involved?
  • What does a router do — where does data flow to and from?
The statement is incorrect. They have different roles:

A modem converts signals from analogue (used on telephone lines) to digital (used by computers), and back again. It connects the home to the internet service provider (ISP).

A router connects different networks together (e.g. your home LAN to the internet WAN). It finds the fastest route for data and directs it to the correct device on your network using IP addresses.

Q4  6 marks

A school wants to upgrade its network. Currently it uses hubs and a single wireless access point in the main building. The school now has two separate buildings 50 metres apart. Students need wireless access throughout both buildings, and staff need a secure, fast wired connection in offices.

Recommend a suitable network setup for the school. Justify your choices of hardware.

  • What should replace the hubs, and why?
  • How do you connect two separate buildings? (Think WAN vs LAN — or is there another solution?)
  • How many WAPs would be suitable?
  • What type of cabling gives the fastest, most secure connection?
  • Is a router needed? What about a firewall?
Replace hubs with switches in both buildings — switches direct data only to the intended device, reducing network congestion and improving speed and security.

Multiple WAPs (at least one per building, possibly more depending on building size) should be installed to ensure full wireless coverage for students. WAPs connected via Ethernet to switches will give reliable wireless coverage.

Wired connections (Ethernet via switches) should be used for staff offices, providing faster and more secure connections than wireless.

A router is needed to connect both buildings' LANs to the internet (WAN) and direct data between networks. It can connect the two buildings either via a direct Ethernet cable (if permitted) or the internet.

A modem will be needed to connect the router to the internet via the ISP.

Network Interface Cards (wired NICs for desktops, wireless NICs for laptops/tablets) are needed in all devices.

Additional credit: Mention of a firewall or VPN between buildings for security; fibre optic cabling for speed between buildings.
